# Result

## Answer
If you want to understand how science works, you could do worse than study the discovery of the dinosaur extinction event. It has everything: a weird accounting discrepancy, a flashy hostile takeover, a decade of people yelling in conference rooms, and—eventually—a very large crater that was, for a while, basically an off-balance-sheet liability sitting under a beach resort.

Also it has dinosaurs, which are like the pre-IPO tech companies of the Mesozoic: dominant, overconfident, and—this is key—operating with way more leverage than they realized.

### The weird line item (1980): Walter Alvarez finds the iridium

In 1980, Walter Alvarez, a geologist working at **Bottaccione Gorge near Gubbio, Italy**, was studying a thin clay layer that marks the **Cretaceous–Paleogene (K–Pg)** boundary—the moment in the rock record when a lot of species vanish. The story begins with a very ordinary academic question: *How long did it take that clay to accumulate?* If you can estimate the deposition rate, you can argue about whether extinctions were sudden or gradual.

So Alvarez teamed up with his father, physicist **Luis Alvarez**, plus **Frank Asaro** and **Helen Michel**, and they applied **neutron activation analysis** to measure trace elements in that boundary clay. And they found something that looks, in retrospect, like the most famous “this doesn’t reconcile” in Earth history: the clay had a spike in **iridium**.

Iridium is not exactly rare in the universe—meteorites have plenty—but it is **rare in Earth’s crust** because it’s a “likes to be in metal” element that mostly wound up in Earth’s core during differentiation. So if you find a sudden iridium enrichment in sediments, it’s like finding a surprise revenue stream that only makes sense if there’s a mysterious outside counterparty.

The Alvarez team’s **Science** paper—**“Extraterrestrial cause for the Cretaceous–Tertiary extinction”** (1980)—reported large iridium increases at the boundary in **Italy, Denmark, and New Zealand**, argued the iridium was **extraterrestrial**, and proposed the now-classic mechanism: a **~10 km asteroid** hits Earth, injects dust into the atmosphere, **blocks sunlight**, and collapses photosynthesis. The dinosaurs, in this telling, didn’t so much “go extinct” as experience a planet-wide margin call.

### The first round of market commentary: “Interesting thesis, but where’s your crater?”

This is the part of the story where you learn that evidence is not the same thing as persuasion.

A lot of scientists thought the impact idea was too neat, too catastrophic, too… cinematic. Geology, like finance, has a long tradition of preferring explanations that feel like boring compounding rather than one-day 80% drawdowns. Plenty of researchers favored more gradual or Earth-internal causes: climate change, sea-level shifts, ecological competition, and—importantly—**massive volcanism**, especially the **Deccan Traps** in India.

Paleontologists also had a practical objection: *In some places, the fossil record makes extinctions look gradual.* Which is true, but also the fossil record is not a Bloomberg terminal; it doesn’t print every trade. In 1982, **Signor and Lipps** formalized a now-standard point: even a sudden extinction can appear gradual because the last fossil you find is usually not the actual last organism that lived (the **Signor–Lipps effect**). This is basically “absence of evidence is not evidence of absence,” but with more stratigraphy.

Meanwhile, impact proponents kept doing what you do in any good investigation: expand the dataset. If the iridium spike shows up globally and at the same horizon, that’s not a quirky local geochemical accident; that’s a worldwide event.

### More exhibits enter the record: shocked quartz, spherules, and other receipts

The impact hypothesis got much stronger as the 1980s progressed because it picked up *diagnostic* evidence—material that is hard to explain any other way.

- **Shocked quartz (1984 and after):** In 1984, **Bohor and colleagues** reported shocked quartz at the boundary in Montana. Quartz grains with planar deformation features are essentially a signature of extreme shock pressures—hypervelocity impact territory. By 1987, shocked quartz had been documented as **globally distributed** in boundary clays. This is less “we have a model” and more “we have a physical artifact from the crime scene.”

- **Spherules/tektites (early hints, then big finds):** Glassy spherules interpreted as impact melt droplets were reported at boundary sites, and by the early 1990s the Caribbean became particularly important. In **Haiti**, researchers reported **tektite-like glasses** and dated K–T boundary tektites using **^40Ar/^39Ar** methods, supporting that the glasses formed right at boundary time.

- **Tsunami/event deposits (near-field chaos):** In places around the Gulf of Mexico, boundary sections include chaotic, high-energy deposits that some researchers interpret as tsunami-related. (As always, sedimentology can be like reading tea leaves: you can argue about storms versus tsunamis, but the *existence* of weird, energetic boundary beds near the Gulf fits nicely with a nearby enormous impact.)

Each of these lines of evidence is like another document subpoenaed in discovery. None alone settles the case for everyone, but together they change the default assumption. By the late 1980s, “something hit Earth” was becoming less an edgy trade and more a consensus position.

And then, inevitably, people asked the question that in finance is called *Where is the money?* and in geology is called *Where is the crater?*

### The missing counterparty: finding Chicxulub

The funny thing is that, in a sense, the crater had been “found” already—it just wasn’t public, and it wasn’t connected to the extinction story.

In **1978**, petroleum geophysicist **Glen Penfield** (working with **Antonio Camargo**) noticed a large circular **gravity/magnetic anomaly** near the town of **Chicxulub** on the Yucatán Peninsula, consistent with a huge buried structure. They presented an abstract about it in **1981** at a Society of Exploration Geophysicists meeting. But oil-company data can be proprietary, and also “giant impact crater under limestone” is not the most obvious first guess when you are trying to find hydrocarbons. It sat, conceptually, like a weird special purpose vehicle: known to a few people, not consolidated into the main story.

In the late 1980s and around **1990**, work on thick impact ejecta deposits (again, the Caribbean matters here) suggested the crater should be in the **Gulf/Caribbean region**. That helped the academic search converge on Yucatán.

In **1991**, things got real fast. A **Nature** note by **Pope, Ocampo, and Duller** pointed to the Yucatán **cenote ring** and geophysical anomalies as evidence of a buried impact structure. And then **Hildebrand and colleagues** published a major **Geology (1991)** paper arguing that Chicxulub is a **~180 km** impact crater and, crucially, tying it to K/T boundary evidence using geophysics and drill data—impact breccias, melt rocks, and shock signatures.

After that, the crater wasn’t just a circle on a gravity map; it was the actual cleared trade. In **1992**, **Kring and Boynton** reported geochemical links between Chicxulub melt rock and K/T spherules in Haiti. Also in **1992**, **Sharpton and colleagues** published additional links, including radiometric age constraints and impact-rock evidence consistent with boundary timing. The crater became the obvious place where the iridium and shocked quartz and glass droplets could have come from.

You can still argue about details—impact versus Deccan volcanism, how much each contributed, the exact kill mechanisms, the duration of ecological collapse. But “a giant asteroid hit near Yucatán at the K–Pg boundary” stopped being a speculative narrative and became the center of gravity.

### A brief meta-commentary on consensus, because that’s what this article is really about

At this point I’m supposed to land the plane with a moral about the scientific method, which always sounds a little like an annual letter from a private equity firm: “We pursued a rigorous, evidence-based process, stress-tested scenarios, and triangulated multiple data sources.” But the dinosaur story actually earns that rhetoric.

The Alvarez team didn’t start with “we want an asteroid.” They started with a timing problem, found an anomalous element, and followed the implications. The community didn’t accept the conclusion just because it was clever; it demanded more: independent replication, better mechanisms, diagnostic shock evidence, and—eventually—the crater itself. People proposed alternatives, fought about them, and then updated their views as the record filled in.

It looks, from the outside, like a sudden revelation: “Oh wow, asteroid, dinosaurs, crater.” But it was really a long due diligence process. The iridium layer was the first red flag in the financial statements. Shocked quartz and tektites were the supporting documents. Chicxulub was the missing counterparty. And once you have the counterparty, the whole set of transactions finally reconciles.

Which is a nice ending, and also a reminder that even the biggest events—mass extinctions, market crashes—are often “discovered” not by one dramatic insight, but by a bunch of people stubbornly adding footnotes until the story can’t not be true.
